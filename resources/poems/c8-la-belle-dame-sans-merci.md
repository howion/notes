# La Belle Dame sans Merci

> By John Keats

<br/>

O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Alone and palely loitering?\
The sedge has withered from the lake,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And no birds sing.

O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;So haggard and so woe-begone?\
The squirrel's granary is full,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And the harvest's done.

I see a lily on thy brow,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;With anguish moist and fever-dew,\
And on thy cheeks a fading rose\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Fast withereth too.

I met a lady in the meads,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Full beautiful—a faery's child,\
Her hair was long, her foot was light,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And her eyes were wild.

I made a garland for her head,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And bracelets too, and fragrant zone;\
She looked at me as she did love,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And made sweet moan

I set her on my pacing steed,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And nothing else saw all day long,\
For sidelong would she bend, and sing\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;A faery's song.

She found me roots of relish sweet,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And honey wild, and manna-dew,\
And sure in language strange she said—\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;"I love thee true".

She took me to her Elfin grot,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And there she wept and sighed full sore,\
And there I shut her wild wild eyes\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;With kisses four.

And there she lullèd me asleep,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And there I dreamed—Ah! woe betide!—\
The latest dream I ever dreamt\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;On the cold hill side.

I saw pale kings and princes too,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Pale warriors, death-pale were they all;\
They cried—"La Belle Dame sans Merci\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Thee hath in thrall!"

I saw their starved lips in the gloam,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;With horrid warning gapèd wide,\
And I awoke and found me here,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;On the cold hill's side.

And this is why I sojourn here,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;Alone and palely loitering,\
Though the sedge is withered from the lake,\
&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;&ensp;And no birds sing.
